Oldstyle Figures vs Lining Figures

10 November 2021

Numbers are very familiar to everyone. Commonly, they are used for dates, times, addresses, measurements, and prices. In typography world, they are called figures or numerals. And, oldstyle figures are one of the two types of numerals, along with lining figures.

Oldstyle Figures

Oldstyle figures (or non-lining, lowercase, hanging, or text figures) have different style and alignments, unlike lining figures, which are built uniformly. Oldstyle figures share the same x-height, ascenders, and descenders with lowercase characters.

Although their name is oldstyle, they are used regularly in modern design. The elegant aesthetic is suitable for running text because their style allows them to blend in with other lowercase characters.

Oldstyle figures can be built with two different spacing styles: proportional and tabular. Proportional oldstyle figures are used for running text, including dates, times, measurements, and pagination. Tabular oldstyle figures are best reserved for tables, financial data, and similar listings.

Lining Figures

Lining figures (also called aligning, cap, or modern figures) are uniform in height, and align with the baseline and the cap height. In some traditional typefaces, certain numerals extend slightly above or below the baseline or the cap height.

Like oldstyle figures, lining figures are also regularly published with two spacing formats, proportional and tabular. Proportional lining figures are well-known for all-cap settings, such as headlines, titles. Tabular lining figures are more preferred for columns of numbers, such as tables, price lists, financial data, and listings.

Conclusion

In my experience, because I am more familiar with modern design, I tend to use lining figures more frequently than the other style. However, learning how to use both styles of figures is crucial for product designers in that they are all effective when used properly in suitable projects.
